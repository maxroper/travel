This week in your travel history
167 countries · 4+ years · raw observations
"Hold memories as important as your savings. There has never been a better time in the history of the world to explore."
Thoughts, patterns, and ideas collected across every continent — written in the field, unfiltered.
Core lessons from the road
01
99% of people are inherently good. Across every culture, religion, and political system — the vast majority of people are kind, curious, and hospitable the moment you show up with respect.
02
Everywhere, people distrust their government. Not in the polarized left-vs-right American way — just a quiet, universal consensus that governments have too much power and are doing a poor job. The US has uniquely weaponized this dissatisfaction into tribalism.
03
Food, drink, and music are the universal language. Every time, without exception, these three things break down barriers faster than anything else. They are the actual fabric connecting people to their culture — not politics, not ideology.
04
Work ethic and honor are dramatically higher elsewhere. The US is focused on the wrong things to sustain innovation and leadership. Countries that combine deregulation with a culture of accountability will likely be next in line.
05
Experiences compound. Material things don't. A new car depreciates the moment you drive it. A trip to a place you've never been rewires how you think — permanently. The return on experience is infinite and the window to take advantage of this moment in history is finite.
06
Being positive matters more than being right. Spending energy arguing about things you can't materially change — climate policy, cultural debates — is a net loss. Across 167 countries, the happiest people are the ones focused outward, not inward on grievance.
07
Whatever country fosters deregulation will lead next. Seen repeatedly: regulation kills innovation. Japan's culture of precision, Southeast Asia's willingness to experiment, the Gulf's ability to build from nothing — the US regulatory environment is quietly strangling its own edge.
08
Identity politics is a uniquely American export — and nobody wants it. Gender ideology, cultural appropriation, and the full suite of woke discourse is not wanted or even understood in most of the world. Everywhere else, people just want their government to work and their families to be safe.
09
Google and US tech are homogenizing the world. The most heartbreaking pattern: extraordinary, ancient cultures slowly replaced by the same iPhone apps and Facebook arguments. When the last distinct culture disappears, something irreplaceable is gone — and it's happening fast.
10
Being wrong and admitting it is a superpower. The US has made it politically impossible to change your mind. Everywhere else, intellectual humility is still respected. The countries that allow it will adapt faster — and win.
Sharp observations
🍸
The Penicillin Problem
Across the best bars on every continent, the Penicillin is the cocktail bartenders default to. It has quietly become the unofficial drink of serious cocktail culture worldwide — often imitated, rarely bettered.
Bars & culture
⭐
The Michelin Blind Spot
The 3-star system — "worth a special journey" — is still built around a French culinary lens. Countless restaurants across Asia, the Middle East, and Latin America deliver transcendent experiences that will never receive recognition simply because they aren't French in technique or format.
Food & dining
🎤
Karaoke Connects Everyone
From Tokyo to Tirana, karaoke is the great equalizer. No language barrier, no political divide survives a room full of people singing badly together. It should be taken more seriously as a diplomatic tool.
Culture
📱
Travel Influencers Are the Worst
The "travel influencer" phenomenon inverts everything travel is supposed to be. The experience becomes secondary to the post. The connection becomes secondary to the content. It is a pyramid scheme where the product being sold is a lifestyle nobody at the bottom will ever actually live.
Modern travel
⚡
Energy Is Everything
Observed in Brazil and across the developing world: power access is the single biggest lever on quality of life, economic growth, and geopolitical power. Solar is nowhere close to enough. The country that solves scalable, cheap, distributed energy wins the next century.
Global systems
🗑️
Trash Is a Bigger Problem Than Carbon
Traveling through Southeast Asia, South America, and Africa — street trash is the most visible and universal crisis. Carbon policy debates feel distant and abstract. A solution that eliminates street-level waste at scale would have more immediate human impact than most climate policy.
Environment
🏛️
Accountability Is the Missing Variable
China as a case study: media exists to hold power accountable. Remove true accountability and financial incentives remain — humans will almost always do the wrong thing. Every failed state has this pattern. Every functioning one has some mechanism of accountability, however imperfect.
Governance
🛐
Dedication Beats Doubt
In Bhutan especially: the dedication that religion provides — something worth fighting for, worth waking up for — is a structural advantage. The US is losing its identity anchors and the consequences are visible. Whatever fills that vacuum matters enormously.
Society
Ideas from the field
🌐
Politician transparency platformHow they voted, what stocks they own, donor lists — all public info, made searchable and digestible. No spin, just facts.
📜
Itemized tax receiptShow every taxpayer exactly which policies their money funds — line by line. Transforms an abstract number into a real accountability tool.
💡
Compact waste incineratorDenmark's incinerator model works at city scale. The opportunity is miniaturizing it — building, neighborhood, or rural community level — for the developing world.
🏥
US medical procedures for exportHighly specific, high-value US medical expertise deployed to markets that desperately need it — not aid, a real business model.
🌿
The next romanticized cropGrapes built entire economies and cultures. Rice built civilizations. What's the next crop that can be elevated — aesthetically, economically, culturally — to that level?
🤖
Truly automated homeA washing machine that folds. A door that's been completely rethought. The home as a system, not a collection of appliances — designed around the life being lived in it.
📦
Non-profit transparency systemPublic-facing financials for charities and NGOs, standardized and searchable. Make impact as verifiable as a financial statement.
🎭
Archive a personCapture someone's stories, voice, and perspective in a format that can be replayed meaningfully by family — not just a recording, a real interactive archive.
🚜
Cheap precision farming toolTargeted at smallholder farmers in the developing world — one device that replaces the most labor-intensive tasks at a price point that actually works in the field.
💰
Future-state VC fundOnly invests in companies whose model depends on technology that is 10 years out — AI, robotics, autonomous logistics. Bet on the world after the current wave, not during it.
🚪
The indestructible doorSecurity is a universal need across every income level. A technologically forward door that actually works for all housing types — not just luxury.
🧠
The next evolution of purposePost-scarcity, post-AI, post-labor: what mental framework do humans need to thrive? The overman question — is it endless desire, or the ability to be genuinely content?
More lessons from the road
11
The USA is still the greatest country. Traveling to 167 nations makes this clearer, not less clear. The privilege of being American — the freedom, the infrastructure, the opportunity — is invisible until you see what most of the world actually lives with.
12
We have lost our way by distracting ourselves with politics. Politics has become entertainment, identity, and religion all at once. The result is that people are spending their finite attention on theater while the actual machinery of their lives goes unexamined.
13
The US runs some of the most sophisticated propaganda in the world — and most citizens don't see it. Having watched state media in 50+ countries, the difference is that ours is wrapped in the aesthetics of independence. CNN still shapes international perception of America, and much of that framing is actively hostile to it.
14
The privilege of working harder than anyone else is ours to keep — or lose. American work ethic, at its best, is still a superpower. The question is whether the next generation will inherit the drive or inherit the entitlement.
15
Islam is widely misunderstood because the loudest 1% define it for the other 99%. The hospitality, generosity, and family-first values of Muslim-majority countries are remarkable. The extremism is real but statistically tiny. The US is increasingly prone to the same dynamic — loud fringe, silent majority.
16
Don't delay experiences. The window is always shorter than it seems. Health changes, circumstances change, the world changes. The memory of something extraordinary compounds for decades. The memory of waiting does not.
17
The most difficult experiences are where you learn the most. Comfort teaches you nothing about yourself. Discomfort — a bad border crossing, an unreliable boat, a place where you can't communicate — that is the curriculum.
18
Commenting on cultures you haven't lived in is a form of ignorance we normalize. It's like saying you know Newport Beach because you've seen pictures. The depth of understanding that comes from being physically present in a place — smelling it, eating it, getting lost in it — cannot be replicated by reading about it.
19
Nobody is thinking about you nearly as much as you think they are. One of travel's great gifts is anonymity. In a country where nobody knows you, you realize how little social surveillance actually exists. The audience you've been performing for mostly isn't watching.
20
There is less meaning in legacy than we are told. Most legacies vanish within two generations. The clearest answer to "what's the point?" isn't impact on others — it's whether you can look at your own life and feel genuine contentment and pride in what you built and experienced.
21
The media is broken everywhere — and disproportionately dangerous where literacy is lowest. In Ethiopia, illiteracy means that whatever is broadcast is accepted as fact. This isn't a third-world problem — it's a scale problem. When the gatekeepers of truth have financial incentives to manufacture outrage, the populations most dependent on them suffer most.
Final thought
"A focus on death always goes to legacy — what you have done, how you have impacted others. But it is just as important to have a good perspective of yourself. What you have done. What you have accomplished. Be content with that."
Friends, family, and experiences are the compound interest of a life well lived. Everything else is noise.
